The Passengers: A Narrative Directory

The Foundation of the Colony

This directory is more than a collection of names and dates. It brings together unvarnished information about the families who endured the first difficult years of Plymouth Colony. While the Mayflower story is often told in broad outlines, the individual lives within it reveal a history marked by hardship, uncertainty, and survival.

Within this company were the "Saints," who sought liberty of conscience, and the "Strangers," who came for opportunity, land, or livelihood. Among them were leaders, servants, laborers, and children, each drawn into a world far harsher than any of them could have expected. From violence and loss to births in the harbor and the struggle to survive the first winter, their stories reflect the resilience required to establish the colony.

This register is intended to show direct biological lines and the spouses who joined them. It offers a closer look at the individuals and households whose lives remain at the center of descendant research today.

The Carver and Robinson Groups (Saints)

  • John Carver (36): Governor and wealthy merchant.
  • Katherine Carver: John's wife.
  • Desire Minter: A young woman and family friend.
  • John Howland (about 21): servant and assistant to John Carver. He famously fell overboard during a storm and survived by grabbing a halyard.
  • Roger Wilder: Servant.
  • William Latham: A young servant boy.
  • Jasper More (7): An indentured child.

The Bradford and Brewster Families (Saints)

  • William Bradford (30): Fustian weaver and future Governor.
  • Dorothy Bradford (23): William's wife.
  • William Brewster (54): The Elder and spiritual leader.
  • Mary Brewster (52): William's wife.
  • Love Brewster (9) and Wrestling Brewster (6): Their young sons.
  • Richard More (6) and Mary More (4): Siblings indentured to the Brewsters.

The Winslow and Allerton Families (Saints)

  • Edward Winslow (25): Printer and diplomat.
  • Elizabeth Winslow: Edward's wife.
  • George Soule (20): A teacher and servant to the Winslows.
  • Isaac Allerton (34): A tailor and businessman.
  • Mary Allerton: Isaac's wife.
  • Bartholomew (8), Remember (5), and Mary (4) Allerton: Their children.
  • John Hooke (13): Isaac's apprentice.

The Fuller and Mullins Families (Saints and Strangers)

  • Samuel Fuller (40): The colony's doctor and a deacon.
  • William White: A Saint and prominent member.
  • Susanna White: William's wife.
  • Resolved White (5): Their son.
  • Peregrine White: The first English child born to the Plymouth settlers in New England (on the ship in the harbor).
  • William Mullins (40): A "Stranger" and shoemaker from London.
  • Alice Mullins: William's wife.
  • Priscilla Mullins (18): Their daughter, who later married John Alden.
  • Joseph Mullins (14): Their son.

The Hopkins and Billington Families (Strangers)

  • Stephen Hopkins (39): Tanner and merchant. The only passenger with previous American experience.
  • Elizabeth Hopkins: Stephen's wife.
  • Giles (12), Constance (11), and Damaris (1) Hopkins: Children from Stephen's first and second marriages.
  • Oceanus Hopkins: Born at sea during the crossing.
  • Edward Doty and Edward Leister: Two rowdy servants to Stephen Hopkins.
  • John Billington (40): Often called the "troublemaker" but a vital survivor.
  • Eleanor Billington: John's wife.
  • John (16) and Francis (14) Billington: Their sons.

The Tilley, Cooke and Warren Families

  • John Tilley (48): Silk worker.
  • Joan Tilley: John's wife.
  • Elizabeth Tilley (13): Their daughter.
  • Edward Tilley (32): John's brother and a cloth worker.
  • Ann Tilley: Edward's wife.
  • Francis Cooke (37): A wool comber.
  • John Cooke (13): Francis's son.
  • Richard Warren (42): A London merchant who traveled alone to scout the land.

  • John Allerton and Thomas English: Two sailors hired to stay with the colony.

The Hired Crew and Others

John Alden and the two sailors mentioned above were officially part of the 102. About 25 to 30 other crew members, including Captain Christopher Jones, stayed on the ship during the first winter but returned to England in the spring of 1621.

The Houses of the Mayflower

The House of Doty

All nine of Edward and Faith's children survived to adulthood, though three grandchildren through their daughter Elizabeth died young.

The Parents: Edward Doty (born about 1599 and died in 1655) arrived as an indentured servant to Stephen Hopkins and was a "Stranger" seeking economic gain. He married his second wife, Faith Clarke (1619 to 1675), in 1635. Faith was the daughter of Thurston and Faith (last name often cited as Loes, though sometimes listed as "Unknown") Clarke.

The Surviving Lineage: All nine children survived: Edward, John, Thomas, Samuel, Desire, Elizabeth, Isaac, Joseph, and Mary.

Position and Land: Doty was a "Purchaser" and shareholder in the colony. By his death, his estate was worth over £137, including sixty acres of upland.

Human Moment: Doty was a firebrand who fought the first duel in New England with a sword and dagger. He appeared in court at least 23 times for everything from slander to "drawing blood".

The House of Hopkins

Those Who Did Not Survive: Oceanus Hopkins, born on the open Atlantic, died in early childhood.

The Parents: Stephen Hopkins (39) was a tanner and merchant who had previously survived a shipwreck in Bermuda. He and his second wife, Elizabeth, both survived the "Starving Time".

The Surviving Lineage: Children from both of Stephen's marriages, Giles (12), Constance (11), and Damaris (1), all survived the first year.

Position and Land: Hopkins was a "Stranger" who held significant social standing and served as a bridge to the Wampanoag people.

Human Moment: Hopkins was once sentenced to death for mutiny in Bermuda after arguing that a governor's authority didn't apply on land.

The House of Billington

Both sons survived the first winter.

The Parents: John Billington (40) was a "Stranger" from London and a signer of the Mayflower Compact. He and his wife, Eleanor, survived the first year.

The Surviving Lineage: Their sons, John (16) and Francis (14), both survived to adulthood.

Position and Land: Often called "troublemakers," they were nevertheless "Purchasers" with a permanent stake in the land.

Human Moment: Young Francis nearly blew up the Mayflower in the harbor when he shot a musket near an open keg of gunpowder.

The House of Brewster

Those Who Did Not Survive: Mary More (4), an indentured child in the home, died during the first winter.

The Parents: William Brewster (54) was the spiritual leader and "Elder". Both he and his wife, Mary (52), survived the "Starving Time".

The Surviving Lineage: Their young sons, Love (9) and Wrestling (6), survived the crossing.

Position and Land: Brewster was university-educated and served as the intellectual heart of Plymouth.

Human Moment: As sickness and death swept through Plymouth, William and Mary Brewster endured while much of the colony seemed to come apart around them. Their household survived to become one of the great enduring lines of Mayflower descent. During the colony's worst illness, Brewster was remembered as one of the few still strong enough to care for the sick and perform even the humblest tasks for those too weak to help themselves.

The House of Carver and Howland

Those Who Did Not Survive: Jasper More (7), an indentured child, died shortly after arrival.

The Parents: John Carver (36) was the first Governor and a wealthy merchant. He and his wife Katherine died in the spring of 1621.

The Surviving Lineage: John Howland (21), Carver's assistant, survived and married survivor Elizabeth Tilley.

Position and Land: Howland eventually became a prominent leader and substantial landowner.

Human Moment: Howland fell overboard during a storm but survived by grabbing a halyard and being hauled back with a boat hook.

The House of White

Those Who Did Not Survive: An unnamed son did not survive the first winter.

The Parents: William White was a "Saint" and signer of the Compact who died in the "Starving Time". His wife, Susanna, survived and later married Edward Winslow.

The Surviving Lineage: Resolved White (5) and Peregrine White (born in the harbor) both survived.

Position and Land: William was a prominent member of the Leiden group.

Human Moment: Susanna White gave birth to Peregrine in the freezing harbor while the colony was being decimated by illness.

The House of Mullins and Alden

Those Who Did Not Survive: Joseph Mullins (14) died during the first winter.

The Parents: William Mullins (40) was a shoemaker and signer of the Compact. He and his wife, Alice, died in the "Starving Time".

The Surviving Lineage: Priscilla Mullins (18) was the sole survivor and married the cooper John Alden (21).

Position and Land: Alden was a key hired man who chose to stay and became a colonial official.

Human Moment: He came as a hired man. She survived the winter that took her father, mother, and brother but went on to be a part of one of the colony's enduring family lines. Together they began one of the most far-reaching Mayflower families in America. Alden had not signed on as a founding patriarch, only as the ship's cooper. He chose to stay, married Priscilla Mullins, and turned a hired voyage into a permanent American line.

The House of Tilley

Those Who Did Not Survive: John Tilley and his wife Joan both died in the "Starving Time". His brother Edward and wife Ann also died that winter.

The Parents: John Tilley (48) and Edward Tilley (32) were signers of the Compact.

The Surviving Lineage: Elizabeth Tilley (13) was the only survivor of her family and eventually married John Howland.

Mayflower Drowning Man Shapes the Course of American History

In 1620, at this time, another disaster struck the storm tossed Mayflower in mid Atlantic. One of the passengers, a servant called John Howland, was swept overboard by a mountainous wave and then miraculously rescued. John Howland went on to be the thirteenth signatory of the Mayflower Compact and was present at the first Thanksgiving. He sired 10 children and had 82 grandchildren. Had he lost his hold and drowned on that fateful day, the two Presidents Bush and President Roosevelt would not have existed as they are all descendants of John Howland and Elizabeth Tilley.

Human Moment: Howland fell overboard during a storm but survived by grabbing a halyard and being hauled back with a boat hook. Bradford wrote that he managed to grab a trailing rope and, though dragged deep beneath the water, held on until the crew pulled him back aboard. That one desperate moment changed the course of his life. Howland survived, later married fellow passenger Elizabeth Tilley, and became the forefather of one of the largest Mayflower lines in America.

The House of Cooke, Rogers, and Warren

The Parents: Francis Cooke (37), Thomas Rogers (48), and Richard Warren (42) were all signers of the Compact. Rogers died during the first winter.

The Surviving Lineage: John Cooke (13), Joseph Rogers (18), and Richard Warren all survived.

Human Moments:

Francis Cooke: Francis Cooke crossed with his young son John while the rest of the family remained behind, waiting to learn whether Plymouth would survive at all. He lived through that uncertainty and made possible a reunion that would turn his line into one of the colony's most enduring. He had to wager an ocean's distance on survival. Only because Francis and John Cooke lived could the family left behind follow them to Plymouth.

Thomas Rogers: Thomas Rogers made the voyage with his son Joseph, but he did not live to see whether the gamble would succeed. He died in the first winter, leaving Joseph to survive in a world his father had barely entered. Joseph Rogers later received land for both himself and his deceased father, a brief record that quietly preserves the cost of Thomas Rogers's journey.

Richard Warren: Richard Warren crossed alone, leaving his wife and daughters behind in England until the colony proved it could endure. Because he survived the colony's first great crisis, his family was later able to join him and begin one of Plymouth's largest descendant lines. His survival turned separation into reunion. Without it, the Warren family's American story would have ended before it began.

The House of Fuller and Eaton

The Parents: Samuel Fuller (40) (doctor), Edward Fuller, and Francis Eaton (carpenter) were all signers of the Compact. Edward and his wife, along with Sarah Eaton, died that winter.

The Surviving Lineage: Samuel Fuller (12) and the infant Samuel Eaton both survived.

Human Moment: Dr. Samuel Fuller was one of the few caregivers well enough to tend the sick during the winter's lowest point.

The House of Chilton and Others

The Parents: James Chilton (64) (the oldest passenger) and Christopher Martin (40) were signers who died that first winter.

The Surviving Lineage: Mary Chilton (13) survived despite losing both parents.

Human Moment: Tradition says Mary Chilton was the first female passenger to step onto Plymouth Rock.

Exact totals for Mayflower descendants are difficult to establish with confidence. Figures such as "8 million," "12 million," or even higher are often broad estimates repeated online rather than totals formally certified by the Silver Books or the General Society of Mayflower Descendants. A more cautious measure is the volume of documented descendant applications, which offers a clearer sense of which lines have been most extensively traced and proven over time.

Some of the most prolific Mayflower lines include:

  • John Alden and Priscilla Mullins.
  • William Brewster and Mary Brewster.
  • John Howland and Elizabeth Tilley.
  • Francis Cooke and John Cooke.
  • Richard Warren.
Research Note:

This directory is meant to assist research and provide historical context. It is interpretive but responsible, genealogically aware but not genealogically exhaustive, and intended to support the main narrative page rather than replace formal lineage sources. In some places, its wording, summaries, or characterizations may not reflect the exact wording, scope, or conclusions of the Silver Books. It should be used as a guide and companion, not as a final authority, and should be read alongside established lineage publications and documented sources.
